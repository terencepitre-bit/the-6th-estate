Former nun details 12 years inside a Carmelite monastery in new memoir

Catherine Coldstream entered a Carmelite monastery at age 24 and spent 12 years living a cloistered life of prayer and obedience before leaving. She recounts her experience in her memoir, Cloistered: My Years as a Nun. Her account offers a rare firsthand perspective on life inside a contemplative religious community and the personal journey that led her to eventually walk away.

Why it matters: First-person accounts of cloistered religious life are rarely made public, and Coldstream's memoir could offer readers an unusually candid window into an institution that affects the lives of thousands of people who enter or consider entering monastic communities.

West Virginia's paid relocation program is turning temporary remote workers into permanent residents

West Virginia's Ascend program, launched during COVID, offered cash incentives to attract remote workers to the state. The initiative is one of dozens of similar relocation programs across the country aimed at providing economic boosts to regions struggling with population and workforce decline. Many participants who originally relocated temporarily have chosen to stay, suggesting these incentive-based programs could be an effective tool for reversing population loss.

Why it matters: For remote workers weighing where to live, paid relocation programs could offer financial support while the communities offering them could see lasting economic and demographic benefits.

Justice Department drops New York Times subpoenas after judicial pushback

The Justice Department has withdrawn subpoenas seeking grand jury testimony and phone records from New York Times journalists, retreating after a federal judge sharply questioned the government's position during a Thursday hearing. The move ends an immediate legal threat to the reporters' sources and communications.

Why it matters: When the government backs down from compelling journalists to testify or hand over records, it could help protect the confidential sources that reporters rely on to bring sensitive information to the public.

Best friends discover they are biological siblings after years of close friendship

Andrea Urmston had long known she had a brother placed with another family through adoption, but had no idea he was already her closest friend. The two had formed a tight bond before uncovering their biological connection. Their story highlights how adoption records and limited family information can leave siblings unaware of each other's existence, sometimes for decades, even when living in close proximity.

Why it matters: For the estimated millions of people separated from siblings through adoption, this case underscores how accessible DNA testing and open adoption records could help families identify connections hiding in plain sight.

Schools Face a Growing Crisis in Teaching Students to Write

As schools have made progress on reading instruction, writing has emerged as the next major literacy challenge. At Sand Creek Elementary in Highlands Ranch, Colorado, teachers are working to explicitly teach students how to compose written work, with some reporting stronger student growth than in previous years. Research suggests that writing instruction has long been inconsistent or undertaught in American classrooms, leaving many students underprepared for academic and professional demands.

Why it matters: Weak writing skills could limit students' academic achievement and career prospects, making the quality of writing instruction in local schools a concern for parents and communities alike.

Rayner Rules Out Rent Controls in England

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner has told the BBC that rent controls will not be introduced in England, arguing that the Renters Rights Act is already having a significant impact on the housing market. Rayner indicated the government is relying on existing legislation rather than price caps to address affordability concerns for tenants across England.

Why it matters: Renters hoping for government-imposed limits on what landlords can charge will need to look to other measures for relief, as rent levels in England could continue to be set by market forces.

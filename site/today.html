Former nun recounts 12 years inside a Carmelite monastery in new memoir

Catherine Coldstream entered a Carmelite monastery at age 24 and spent 12 years living a cloistered life of prayer and obedience before leaving. She has written about the experience in her memoir, Cloistered: My Years as a Nun, offering a firsthand account of what life inside such a community could look like for those who commit to it.

Why it matters: Firsthand accounts from people who have left religious institutions could offer rare insight into cloistered communities that are otherwise closed to public view.

West Virginia's paid relocation program is turning temporary remote workers into permanent residents

West Virginia's Ascend program, launched during COVID, offered cash incentives to attract digital nomads to the state. The initiative is one of dozens of similar relocation programs across the country designed to give economically struggling areas a boost. The program appears to be achieving its goals, with a notable share of participants choosing to stay long-term rather than moving on.

Why it matters: Relocation incentive programs could expand to more states, meaning remote workers may increasingly find themselves eligible for cash payments simply for choosing where to live and work.

Sources: West Virginia paid digital nomads to move there during COVID. Many are staying

Justice Dept. drops New York Times subpoenas after judicial pushback

The Justice Department has withdrawn subpoenas seeking grand jury testimony and phone records from New York Times journalists. The decision came after a federal judge sharply questioned the government's pursuit of the records during a Thursday hearing. The move ends an effort to compel journalists to reveal information that could have exposed confidential sources.

Why it matters: When the government withdraws subpoenas targeting journalists under court pressure, it could signal limits on how far federal investigators can go in compelling reporters to disclose their sources.

Sources: Justice Dept. drops 'New York Times' subpoenas under pressure from judge

Elon Musk, speaking to The Economist, said he is unconcerned about public criticism and denied being a racist. Schools face a new literacy frontier: teaching students to write

As reading instruction reforms gain traction, educators are turning attention to a parallel gap: writing. At Sand Creek Elementary in Highlands Ranch, Colorado, first grade teachers are using structured writing lessons to build composition skills early, with some teachers reporting stronger student growth than in previous years. Researchers and educators increasingly argue that writing instruction has long been underfunded and under-prioritized in American classrooms.

Why it matters: Students who leave school without strong writing skills could face lasting disadvantages in higher education and the workforce, where clear written communication is a core requirement across nearly every field.

Sources: The next literacy challenge: Teaching students how to write

Rayner Rules Out Rent Controls in England

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner has told the BBC that rent controls will not be introduced in England, citing the Renters Rights Act as already having a significant impact on the housing market. Rayner indicated the government believes existing legislation is sufficient to address tenant concerns, closing the door on a policy some housing advocates had pushed for as a direct check on rising rental costs.

Why it matters: Renters hoping for government caps on how much landlords can charge will need to look to existing protections under the Renters Rights Act rather than any new price controls, which could limit their options if rents continue to rise.

Sources: Angela Rayner rules out rent controls in England

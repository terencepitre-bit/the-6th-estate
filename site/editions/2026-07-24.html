Former nun recounts 12 years of cloistered life in new memoir

Catherine Coldstream entered a Carmelite monastery at age 24 and spent 12 years living under strict religious vows before eventually leaving. Her memoir, Cloistered: My Years as a Nun, details her experience of prayer and obedience inside the monastery walls. Coldstream has since spoken publicly about what led her to walk away from that life.

Why it matters: First-person accounts of cloistered religious life are rare, and Coldstream's story could offer readers an uncommon window into institutions that typically remain hidden from public view.

West Virginia's paid relocation program draws remote workers — and many are staying

West Virginia's Ascend program offered cash incentives to attract remote workers during COVID, and it is one of dozens of similar initiatives launched across the country to boost local economies. The program appears to be achieving lasting results, with many participants choosing to remain in the state long-term rather than treating the incentive as a short-term gain.

Why it matters: Relocation incentive programs could offer remote workers a financial pathway to lower costs of living while helping struggling communities rebuild their economies.

Justice Department drops subpoenas against New York Times journalists

The Justice Department has withdrawn subpoenas demanding grand jury testimony and phone records from New York Times journalists. The move came after a federal judge sharply questioned the government's position during a Thursday hearing. The department's reversal ends an immediate legal threat to the reporters involved.

Why it matters: When the government backs down from subpoenaing journalists, it could help protect reporters' ability to gather news from confidential sources without fear of compelled disclosure.

Sources: Justice Dept. drops 'New York Times' subpoenas under pressure from judge

Best friends discover they are biological siblings after years-long bond

Andrea Urmston had always known she had a brother placed with another family through adoption, but did not know his identity. After forming a close friendship, the two discovered they were the biological siblings they had each been searching for. The reunion highlights how adoption records and limited disclosure policies can leave siblings unaware they are living in proximity to their closest relatives.

Why it matters: For adoptees and birth families, the story underscores how little information many people have about biological relatives, and could prompt individuals in similar situations to explore legal avenues for accessing adoption records.

Sources: They became best friends - then discovered they were brother and sister

Schools turn focus to writing instruction after reading reform gains

As schools have made progress tackling the reading crisis, educators are identifying writing as the next major literacy gap. At Sand Creek Elementary in Highlands Ranch, Colorado, teachers are implementing structured writing lessons starting in kindergarten and first grade, guiding young students through composing paragraphs step by step. Teachers report seeing stronger writing growth with more explicit instruction, suggesting that direct teaching methods similar to those used in reading reform could produce gains in student writing skills.

Why it matters: Weak writing skills could limit students' academic success and long-term career prospects, making early, structured writing instruction a consequential shift for families to watch in their local schools.

Sources: The next literacy challenge: Teaching students how to write

Rayner Rules Out Rent Controls in England

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner has told the BBC that rent controls will not be introduced in England, citing the Renters Rights Act as already delivering significant impact on the housing market. Rayner indicated the government considers existing legislation sufficient to address tenant concerns, signalling that further regulatory intervention on pricing is not planned for the foreseeable future.

Why it matters: Renters hoping for government caps on rising rental costs could need to look to existing tenant protections rather than new pricing regulations for relief.

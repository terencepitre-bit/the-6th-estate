Oil hits $100 a barrel as Houthi attacks and new Trump tariffs shake markets

Oil surged to $100 per barrel after Iran-backed Houthi rebels attacked two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea, rattling global energy markets. Simultaneously, President Trump replaced a set of expiring tariffs with a new round of trade levies. The twin developments signal rising pressure on global supply chains and energy costs, with both geopolitical tension and trade policy tightening in the same window.

Why it matters: Higher oil prices and new tariffs could push up costs for everyday goods, from fuel at the pump to imported products, squeezing household budgets.

West Virginia's paid relocation program is turning temporary remote workers into permanent residents

West Virginia's Ascend program, launched during COVID, offered cash incentives to attract digital nomads to the state. The initiative is one of dozens of similar relocation programs across the country designed to provide economic boosts to communities in need. The program appears to be achieving longer-term results, with a notable share of participants choosing to remain in the state beyond their initial relocation period.

Why it matters: For remote workers weighing where to live, paid relocation programs could offer financial support while opening access to lower costs of living — and for struggling communities, retained residents could mean sustained economic growth.

Sources: West Virginia paid digital nomads to move there during COVID. Many are staying
Business / Policy

Justice Dept. drops subpoenas targeting New York Times journalists after judicial pushback

The Justice Department has withdrawn subpoenas that sought grand jury testimony and phone records from New York Times journalists. The decision came after a federal judge sharply questioned the government's position during a Thursday hearing. The move ends an immediate legal threat to the reporters, though the broader tension between federal investigations and press freedom remains unresolved.

Why it matters: When the government backs down from demanding journalists' records, it could help protect reporters' ability to gather news from confidential sources without fear of exposure.

Sources: Justice Dept. drops 'New York Times' subpoenas under pressure from judge

Musk Shrugs Off Public Backlash as AI Concerns Grow

Elon Musk, speaking to The Economist, said he does not care if people hate him and denied being a racist. His comments come amid mounting public fears about artificial intelligence. Musk urged an attitude of embracing the moment, saying 'let's enjoy the ride.' The interview signals that Musk remains unbothered by reputational pressure even as scrutiny of his influence over AI development intensifies.

Why it matters: Musk's dismissiveness toward public concern could shape how the broader AI industry responds to calls for accountability and caution.

Education

Schools face a growing push to teach writing with the same rigor applied to reading

A Colorado elementary school is among those rethinking how writing is taught to young students, with teachers reporting stronger student progress when explicit, structured writing instruction is used. The effort mirrors the recent science-of-reading movement that reshaped how schools approach literacy, and educators say writing has long been undertaught compared to reading. First-grade students at Sand Creek Elementary in suburban Denver are practicing foundational paragraph-building skills as part of the shift.

Why it matters: Students who do not receive strong early writing instruction could struggle with communication skills that affect academic performance, job prospects, and civic participation throughout their lives.

Sources: The next literacy challenge: Teaching students how to write

Rayner Rules Out Rent Controls in England

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner has told the BBC that rent controls will not be introduced in England, citing the Renters Rights Act as already delivering significant impact on the housing market. Rayner's comments appear to close the door on a policy some housing advocates have pushed for as a way to ease affordability pressures on tenants facing rising rents across the country.

Why it matters: Renters hoping for government caps on rent increases could face continued market-rate rises, making it harder for lower-income households to secure or remain in stable housing.

Sources: Angela Rayner rules out rent controls in England

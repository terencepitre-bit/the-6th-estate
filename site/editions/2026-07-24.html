Former nun recounts 12 years inside a Carmelite monastery in new memoir

Catherine Coldstream entered a Carmelite monastery at age 24 and spent 12 years living a cloistered life of prayer and obedience before leaving. She has written about her experience in a memoir titled Cloistered: My Years as a Nun, offering a rare firsthand account of life inside a closed religious community.

Why it matters: First-person accounts of cloistered religious life are uncommon, and Coldstream's memoir could offer readers an unusually candid look at the psychological and spiritual realities of monastic commitment.

West Virginia's paid relocation program is turning temporary remote workers into long-term residents

Ascend West Virginia, launched during the COVID pandemic, offered cash incentives to attract remote workers to the state seeking an economic boost. The program is one of dozens of similar relocation initiatives operating across the country. Early results suggest the strategy is working, with a notable share of participants choosing to stay beyond their initial commitment, signaling that financial incentives could reshape population trends in struggling regions.

Why it matters: For remote workers weighing a move, these programs could mean thousands in relocation cash while potentially revitalizing communities that offer lower costs of living.

Justice Department drops New York Times subpoenas after judicial pushback

The Justice Department has withdrawn subpoenas seeking grand jury testimony and phone records from New York Times journalists. The move came after a federal judge sharply questioned the government's position during a Thursday hearing. The department's retreat marks a significant reversal in what had been a closely watched confrontation between federal prosecutors and a major news organization over reporter records.

Why it matters: When prosecutors seek journalists' phone records and testimony, it could expose confidential sources and deter whistleblowers from speaking to the press, affecting the public's access to government accountability reporting.

Best friends discover they are biological siblings after years of close friendship

Andrea Urmston had grown up knowing she had a brother who had been adopted by another family. Schools Face a New Literacy Frontier: Teaching Students to Write

As schools have made progress on reading instruction, writing has emerged as the next major challenge in early education. At Sand Creek Elementary in Highlands Ranch, Colorado, first grade teachers are working to build foundational writing skills in young students, guiding them through structured composition exercises. Educators report seeing measurable growth when explicit writing instruction is prioritized, suggesting that focused classroom approaches could make a meaningful difference in student outcomes.

Why it matters: Students who do not develop strong writing skills early could face lasting disadvantages in academic performance, standardized testing, and eventually workplace communication.

Sources: The next literacy challenge: Teaching students how to write

Rayner Rules Out Rent Controls in England

Housing Secretary Angela Rayner has told the BBC that rent controls will not be introduced in England, arguing that the Renters Rights Act is already having a significant impact on the housing market. Rayner's comments signal the government's position that existing legislation is sufficient to address affordability concerns, closing the door on a policy some tenant advocates have pushed for as rents remain elevated across much of England.

Why it matters: Renters hoping for government-imposed caps on rental costs could now need to look to the Renters Rights Act alone for relief, meaning landlords retain the ability to set market-rate rents across England.

Sources: Angela Rayner rules out rent controls in England
